The Coming Age of Technology ~ 1996

March 4, 2026

Before reading this article, I had never really considered how important "calm" is as a design goal in a world where technology surrounds us constantly. The idea that technology should move between the center and periphery of our attention felt especially new to me, because I have grown up in a world where devices usually demand full and constant focus. What stood out most is that calm technology is not anti-technology at all. It still informs us, but it does so without overwhelming us, letting information sit in the background until we actually need it.

The reading also made me think differently about what technology in the home is supposed to feel like. My first real glimpse of what tech could look like in our homes 24/7 was a Samsung smart fridge, and as a little girl I always wanted one. It seemed futuristic and exciting. But now I see why I would probably be just as happy with a normal fridge, where I am not being bombarded with messages, updates, and a screen when I am just trying to get a glass of water in the middle of the night. That example made the reading feel real to me, because it showed how easily convenience can turn into constant interruption.

Overall, this reading made me think that the real challenge is not just creating more advanced technology, but designing it in a way that supports human attention rather than competing for it. It also made me rethink the shift from "one person, one computer" to a world where many technologies quietly surround us all the time. I find that idea both interesting and a little unsettling. It makes me curious whether technology can ever truly become calm in a culture that is so dependent on distraction, and what it would look like if designers really prioritized that kind of experience.
